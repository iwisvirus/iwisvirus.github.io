After graduating from the University of Toronto with a Computer Science degree, I was struggling to figure out what I wanted to do. Then I ended up taking Google's cybersecurity course. After the first couple of videos, I knew this was going to be my career. I had never felt so excited to go home and start the next lesson. Every time I learned something new, I wanted to learn even more.

For me, digital forensics is like an onion (a well-known quote from Shrek). The more layers you peel back, the more there is underneath. The deeper I went, the more fascinated I became. There were several reasons why I fell in love with this field.

I have always been driven by curiosity and a desire to investigate complex problems. For me, it has always been about the process and the "why" rather than simply getting a good result. This applies to both logical and ethical questions. On the logical side, many of my computer science courses challenged me to think critically and solve difficult problems. Outside of school, I enjoy playing chess and complex board games that require strategy and analysis.

On the ethical side, I have always spent a lot of time thinking about values, morality, and personal growth. I am someone who constantly tries to become a better version of myself. My competition has never been me versus the world; it has always been me yesterday versus me today. When you think deeply about life, you end up asking a lot of "why" questions. Why do people make the choices they do? Why are certain values important? Why do societal norms exist? Understanding these things requires problem-solving, analysis, and curiosity.

Coming from someone whose brain works this way, digital forensics felt like the perfect fit. The combination of investigation, critical thinking, ethics, technology, and understanding the "why" behind events felt incredibly fulfilling. It still does.

As I continued learning, I realized that digital forensics was more than just solving technical puzzles. What really drew me in was the purpose behind the work. Digital forensics has the potential to help real people. Whether it is supporting victims, assisting investigations, uncovering the truth, or providing answers during difficult situations, the work has a meaningful impact on society.

I started developing a strong sense of responsibility toward the field. Every artifact, log entry, deleted file, or recovered piece of evidence tells part of a story. As forensic analysts, we have the opportunity to piece those stories together and help uncover the truth. Knowing that my work could contribute to justice, accountability, and helping others transformed digital forensics from something I enjoyed into something I genuinely care about. The technical challenges keep me engaged, but the opportunity to make a difference is what gives the work meaning.

I have also always been fascinated by investigations. I grew up watching CSI, crime documentaries, and investigative videos on YouTube. Name a criminal documentary, and there's a good chance I've watched it. I also have a habit of digging into things. If I want to understand something, I will keep asking questions and digging deeper until I find answers.

Maybe that is not always the best habit in everyday life, but in digital forensics, it feels like a strength. Tracing someone's actions through digital evidence, connecting small pieces of information, reconstructing timelines, figuring out what happened, and understanding why it happened is incredibly rewarding to me. Then taking all of that information and turning it into a clear, well-written report feels like the final piece of the puzzle. That is when I knew digital forensics was the path I wanted to pursue.

During my search for mentors, one mentor asked me a simple question:

"What is something you could do for eight hours straight without getting bored?"

The answer came to me immediately: digital forensics.

It is the career I genuinely want to pursue. It challenges me intellectually, satisfies my curiosity, aligns with my values, and gives me the opportunity to contribute something meaningful.

For anyone interested in this field, if you enjoy investigating, solving problems, understanding why things happen, and helping people through your work, I would encourage you to explore digital forensics. And if you find something you are truly passionate about, do not be afraid to reach out to people who share that passion. In my experience, passionate people recognize passion in others, and many of them are willing to help.

Without those people, I would have been very lost in my journey.

Thanks for reading about why I love digital forensics. I'll be yapping about some of the investigations, tools, and projects I've worked on in future posts.
